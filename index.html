The Problem with Interviews

Interviews can be unpredictable. This workshop aims to demystify the tech interview process.

Interviews can often feel unpredictable, leaving candidates unsure of what to expect. This workshop is designed to demystify the tech interview process, helping you understand what interviewers are really looking for and how you can best prepare. By the end of this session, you'll have a clearer idea of how to approach technical interviews with confidence. I interviewed several hiring managers to understand what they look for in candidates. This is what they had to say.

Expectations Based on Level

I interviewed several hiring managers to understand what they look for in candidates:

  • All Levels: Showcase your work publicly; employers value this.
  • Junior Level: Soft skills are crucial.
  • Mid Level: Demonstrate your technical growth and adaptability.
  • Senior Level: Preparedness and deep knowledge are key. Present at conferences, write articles, and align with current practices.
Across all levels, it's crucial to have a public portfolio, whether it's a GitHub repository or a personal blog, as employers are increasingly looking for evidence of practical work. For junior candidates, it's essential to focus on soft skills like communication, teamwork, and problem-solving, which are often more critical than deep technical knowledge at this stage. Mid-level candidates should emphasize their technical growth and adaptability by showcasing how they've learned new technologies or taken on greater responsibilities. For senior-level candidates, preparedness and deep knowledge are key. It's important to position yourself as a thought leader by presenting at conferences or writing technical articles, which can really set you apart. This insight comes from David, a Senior Director in IT, who highlighted the importance of showcasing your work publicly, regardless of your level, as it can open up valuable networking opportunities for your career.

Interview Insights

M. Noonan

  • Alignment is crucial in the initial interview phase.
  • Focus on problem-solving and communication over technical speed in tech screens.
  • "Grit, Game, & Growth" are key strengths: perseverance, collaboration, and continuous development.
  • Curiosity and humility are highly valued traits.
  • Avoid candidates who are unkind or lack the ability to contextualize their work within a team.
Mark Noonan emphasizes the importance of aligning the candidate's experience and interests with the role from the outset. He values problem-solving and communication during tech interviews, rather than just technical ability. The key strengths he looks for are encapsulated in the phrase "Grit, Game, & Growth," focusing on perseverance, collaboration, and ongoing development. He also values curiosity and humility, and is cautious of candidates who are unkind or cannot contextualize their work as part of a team.

Interview Insights

Eddie

  • Use standard language libraries during interviews.
  • Start with a docstring to outline the solution: define inputs and outputs before coding.
  • Follow "top down" programming approach.
  • Practice idiomatic programming (e.g., dictionary and list comprehensions).
  • Using AI: Try solving with an algorithm first, then use AI to learn and refine.
Eddie emphasizes the importance of using standard language libraries and following best practices like starting with a docstring to define inputs and outputs before coding. He advocates for a "top down" programming approach, which involves structuring the solution from a high level before diving into the details. Additionally, Eddie highlights the value of idiomatic programming, using tools like dictionary and list comprehensions to write clean and efficient code. He also supports the idea of trying to solve problems with a top-down programming approach first and, then using AI to learn and improve your approach, as a good strategy for learning.

  • Engage with the Community: Encourage feedback and discussion by sharing your blog posts on social media and developer communities (like CodeBar!). Interaction with others can lead to new ideas and perspectives.
  • Example of Learning in Public: Kedasha Kerr (from Social Worker to Software Engineer at Microsoft): itsthatlady.dev

Writing tutorials or guides based on your experiences not only reinforces your own knowledge but also helps others who are on a similar path. Study Tips Using AI

  • Do NOT: copy and paste code you should understand (Ex: algorithms & data structures)
  • Do: Use AI to quiz yourself on topics.
  • Do: Create a study plan with AI (e.g., "Learn Python in 5 hours").
  • Do: Understand the topics deeply to discuss them confidently in an interview.
  • Do: Spend a block of your time using active recall to write and rewrite code from memory generated by AI.
It's importance of not just copying code but truly understanding it, especially when dealing with challenging subjects like algorithms and data structures. I suggest using AI as a tool to create self-quizzes, which can help reinforce knowledge and identify areas that need more focus. I recommend leveraging AI to develop customized study plans, particularly for efficient learning within a set timeframe. Coders need a deep understanding of topics to perform well in interviews, where candidates must confidently explain concepts. Active recall is a powerful technique for solidifying knowledge. All coders should practice writing code from memory to enhance retention.

How to Learn New Technologies Quickly

  • Start with the "Hello World" tutorial.
  • Find a book (free resources are great).
  • Apply the Pareto Principle—focus on the 20% of topics that give you 80% of the results.

When learning a new technology, it's important to start with something simple like a "Hello World" tutorial, which provides a quick win and a basic understanding of the setup. Next, finding a comprehensive resource, such as a book or a well-structured online course, can help build a solid foundation. Free resources are particularly valuable and often sufficient. Finally, applying the Pareto Principle can accelerate learning—focus on mastering the essential 20% of the technology that will deliver 80% of the benefits. This approach allows for quicker application and understanding, making you more productive with the new technology in a shorter amount of time.
